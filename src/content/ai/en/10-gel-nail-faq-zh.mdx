## FAQ 1: How long do gel nails last?

**Answer:** 2–4 weeks, depending on technique, aftercare habits, and gel brand.

According to Hanyi Nails (June 2025): "Gel nails typically last 2–4 weeks, depending on factors such as brand quality, nail preparation, and post-application care." Premium brands like CND, OPI, and Gelish typically fall on the longer end of that range.

**Key factors affecting longevity:**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Nail prep quality | High — proper filing and degreasing = stronger adhesion |
| Gel brand quality | High — premium brands last 3–4 weeks; budget products may lift within a week |
| Post-care habits | High — gloves, cuticle oil, no picking |
| Lifestyle / hand use | Medium — heavy manual work shortens wear |
| Application technique | High — sealing the free edge reduces lifting |

## FAQ 2: Will gel nails damage my nails?

**Answer:** Gel is gentler than acrylic, but damage is real — and the main cause isn't the gel itself, it's removal.

When properly applied and removed, gel causes **minimal damage**. The real risks come from:

1. **Incorrect removal** — peeling off without soaking is the #1 cause of gel-related nail damage
2. **Over-filing during application** — filing too aggressively thins the nail plate
3. **Wearing gel too long** — wearing continuously beyond 4 weeks increases the risk of fungal infection

As Amoure Nail (October 2025) put it: "Damage comes from peeling or over-buffing — not from our gentle soak-off removal process."

**How to minimize damage:**
- Always soak off — never peel or force removal
- Choose a qualified technician — don't cut corners with inexperienced ones
- Rest your nails for 1–2 weeks between full sets
- Never wear gel continuously for more than 4 weeks

## FAQ 3: Is the UV lamp dangerous?

**Answer:** UV exposure from a single nail appointment is very low. For occasional use, the risk is minimal.

UV nail lamps emit UVA light (320–400nm wavelength). Prolonged, frequent exposure theoretically could accelerate skin aging and carries an extremely low cancer risk.

However, a 2023 study published in *Nature Communications* found no significant association between UV nail lamp use and cancer risk for occasional users. The FDA considers them safe when used as directed.

**Practical tips:**
- Prefer LED lamps — they produce less UVA exposure than traditional UV lamps
- Apply SPF 30+ sunscreen to your hands before curing, or use fingerless UV-protective gloves
- If you're concerned about frequency, limit sessions to no more than every 2–3 weeks
- The medical consensus is that occasional gel manicures pose negligible cancer risk

## FAQ 4: How long does curing take?

**Answer:** 30 seconds to 2 minutes per layer; a full set is done in under 15 minutes.

According to Reforma EU (May 2025): "With the right tools and technique, complete curing can be achieved in under 15 minutes."

| Layer | UV lamp (36W) | LED lamp (48W) |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Base coat | 30–60 sec | 20–30 sec |
| Color gel | 60–120 sec | 30–60 sec |
| Top coat | 60–120 sec | 30–60 sec |
| **Full set, both hands** | **10–15 min** | **5–10 min** |

Insufficient curing causes sticky, undercured layers at the base — leading to early lifting and cracking. Always follow the recommended curing times for your specific lamp and gel brand.

## FAQ 5: Can I wear regular nail polish over gel?

**Answer:** Yes — but regular nail polish cannot go under a UV lamp.

You can absolutely apply regular nail polish on top of gel, with these caveats:

- **Never** put regular nail polish under a UV/LED lamp — it will bubble, crack, or discolor
- Apply as normal: base coat first, then color
- Use non-acetone nail polish remover to remove it — acetone can damage the gel top coat
- Layering regular polish over gel gives you more color options without redoing a full gel set

## FAQ 6: Why does my gel lift after just a few days?

**Answer:** Usually one of four causes: poor nail prep, no free edge sealing, contact with water too soon, or insufficient curing.

**Most common reasons for lifting:**

| Cause | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| **Nails not degreased** — oils block gel adhesion | Request alcohol wipe + degreaser before application |
| **Free edge not sealed** — gel only on top, not wrapped around the tip | Every layer should wrap around the free edge |
| **Water contact too soon** — gel needs 24 hours to fully cure | Avoid washing dishes or swimming for 24 hours after |
| **Insufficient curing** — base layer is still soft | Choose a salon with properly calibrated lamps |
| **Low-quality gel** — poor adhesion formula | Ask for salon-grade gel (CND, OPI, Gelish) |

## FAQ 7: Can I remove gel nails at home?

**Answer:** Yes, with the right tools and patience — though salon removal is generally safer.

**Safe at-home removal steps:**
1. Use a 180-grit nail file to buff off the shiny top coat
2. Soak cotton pads in 100% pure acetone
3. Wrap each finger with aluminum foil, let sit for 15–20 minutes
4. Use a wooden stick to gently push off the softened gel
5. Lightly buff away any remaining residue

**Go to a salon instead if:**
- You've worn the gel for more than 4 weeks (nails may be thinned)
- You notice green, black, or dark discoloration under the gel (possible fungal infection — see a doctor first)
- Your nails feel thin, painful, or overly sensitive

## FAQ 8: How often should I get gel nails?

**Answer:** Every 2–3 weeks for fills is the standard for most people.

The ideal schedule depends on:
- **Nail growth rate:** Fast growers will see the fill line appear sooner
- **Lifestyle:** Water and hand use frequency affects how long the gel stays looking good
- **Nail health:** If you're experiencing thinning, sensitivity, or lifting, extend the interval

**General recommendations:**
- Fills: every 2–3 weeks
- Full sets: maximum every 4 weeks (never exceed 4 weeks)
- Nail rest periods: at least 1–2 weeks between full sets

## FAQ 9: Is it safe to get gel nails while pregnant?

**Answer:** Generally considered safe with some precautions, though no definitive research exists.

There's no clear evidence that gel manicures harm pregnant women. Key considerations:

- **UV exposure:** Occasional UV lamp use is low risk, but you can apply sunscreen or wear fingerless UV gloves
- **Chemical fumes:** Some pregnant women are more sensitive to gel product scents — ensure the salon is well-ventilated
- **Acetone:** Brief skin contact with pure acetone is safe, but avoid prolonged soaking
- **Skin sensitivity:** Pregnancy can make nails and skin more reactive — watch for allergic reactions

Most OBGYNs consider gel manicures safe when done in a well-ventilated space, no more than every 3–4 weeks, with minimal UV exposure.

## FAQ 10: What's the difference between gel, Shellac, and hard gel?

**Answer:** Shellac is a brand (by CND); hard gel is a material type; soft gel is what most people mean by "gel nails."

| Type | Material | Curing | Feel | Best for |
|------|----------|--------|------|----------|
| **Shellac** | CND's proprietary gel-polish hybrid | LED, 30–60 sec/layer | Ultra-thin, flexible, natural | Light coverage, natural look |
| **Soft gel (regular gel)** | Soft gel polymer | UV or LED, 30–120 sec/layer | Soft, comfortable | Full color coverage |
| **Hard gel** | Cross-linked polymer, no monomer | UV or LED, 2–5 min/layer | Very hard, high strength | Major extensions, nail sculpting |
| **Acrylic** | Acrylic powder + liquid monomer | Air-dry, no lamp needed | Hard, rigid | Long extensions, maximum durability |

Shellac is technically a type of soft gel, but CND markets it as its own product line due to its proprietary formula and nearly no-filing application method.

## FAQ 13: Can I do gel nails at home?

**Basic at-home gel supplies:**
- LED/UV lamp (48W or above, with timer)
- Base coat, gel color, top coat (all three are essential)

**Beginner tips:**

- Practice on a few fingers first to get a feel for curing times
- Never skip base coat and top coat — they're critical for longevity
- Watch YouTube tutorials — cat eye and watercolor effects require specific techniques

## FAQ 15: Will gel nails make my nails thinner?

**Answer:** Not with proper technique — but continuous long-term wear and over-filing will noticeably thin the nail plate.

**What causes gel to thin nails:**

| Cause | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Aggressive filing each session | Nail plate thins and weakens |
| Peeling off gel instead of soaking | Surface of nail plate is stripped |
| Wearing gel longer than 4 weeks | Increased fungal/bacterial infection risk |
| Never taking nail rest periods | Nails can't recover thickness |

**How to protect your nails:**
- Choose a skilled technician who files conservatively
- Request soak-off removal only — never mechanical peeling
- Take 1–2 week breaks between full sets
- Use nail strengthening oil during the gel period (e.g., OPI Nail Envy)

## FAQ 17: What is cuticle oil and do I need it after gel?

**Answer:** Cuticle oil moisturizes the skin around your nails — applying it daily after gel is the cheapest way to make your manicure last longer.

**What cuticle oil does:**
- Softens and hydrates the cuticle area, preventing overgrowth
- Keeps the skin around your nails flexible, reducing cracking
- The oil lightly lubricates the gel edge, reducing lifting

**How to use it:**
- Apply once before bed (most effective — your hands have time to absorb it)
- Reapply after washing dishes or using cleaning products
- Massage gently into the nail base for 30 seconds after applying

**Recommended frequency:**
- While wearing gel: once or twice daily
- During nail rest periods: once daily
- If cuticles are very dry: 3–4 times daily

## FAQ 18: What should I do if my gel peels or has bubbles?

**Answer:** Peeling and bubbles usually indicate application errors. Minor cases can be patched; severe cases need a redo.

**Common causes of lifting (gel separating from the nail):**

| Cause | Solution |
|-------|----------|
| Insufficient curing (gel not fully dry) | Re-cure for 30–60 sec |
| Oils not cleaned off the nail surface | Remove and redo — never skip degreasing |
| Color gel applied too thick | Thin coats, cure each layer fully |
| Gel touching skin or cuticles | Application error — needs to be redone |

**Emergency home fixes:**
- If only a small corner is lifting, you can apply a layer of clear top coat to seal it temporarily
- If more than 1/3 of the nail has lifted, book a removal and redo
- Never ignore lifting — moisture can get trapped underneath and cause fungal growth

## FAQ 19: Gel nails vs. acrylic — which is better?

**Answer:** Depends on what you want. Gel is better for a natural look and faster appointments; acrylic is better for dramatic length and maximum durability.

| Feature | Gel | Acrylic |
|---------|-----|---------|
| **Wear time** | 2–4 weeks | 3–6 weeks |
| **Feel** | Soft, glossy | Hard, rigid |
| **Nail impact** | Gentler | More damaging |
| **Removal** | Acetone soak (15–20 min) | Diamond file / solvent |
| **Application time** | 1–2 hours | 1.5–2.5 hours |
| **Odor** | Very faint | Strong monomer smell |
| **Max extension length** | Medium (up to ~0.5–1 inch) | Very long (2 inches+) |
| **Best for** | Natural look, easy maintenance | Very short nails, extreme length |

From 2024–2026, gel's market share has continued rising, while acrylic holds steady among clients who want maximum extension or specific artistic effects.

## FAQ 20: How do I make my gel nails last longer? 10 care tips

**Answer:** Gel's enemies are water, cleaning products, physical impact, and time — protecting your manicure starts with daily habits.

**10 tips to extend gel nail life:**

1. **No water contact for 24 hours after application** — gel needs at least a full day to completely cure
2. **Wear gloves when washing dishes** — dish soap is the #1 gel killer
3. **Use cuticle oil daily** — keeps the edges sealed
4. **Don't use your nails as tools** — use keys, pens, or actual tools instead
5. **Avoid extreme heat** — hot water and saunas can prematurely soften gel
6. **Seal minor lifting immediately** — apply clear top coat at the first sign of a lifted edge
7. **Choose LED over UV** — more even curing, better adhesion
8. **Always seal the free edge** — wrap each layer around the tip of the nail
9. **Book removal within 3 weeks** — don't wait until 4+ weeks
10. **Choose a skilled technician** — nail prep accounts for about 50% of how long gel lasts
