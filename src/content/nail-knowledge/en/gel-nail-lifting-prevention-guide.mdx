Your gel manicure is barely two weeks old, and you can already see the edge starting to lift. Frustrating, right?

Most people blame the nail technician — but the truth is, about half of a gel manicure's lifespan depends on what you do at home. Here are the 3 real reasons gel nails lift prematurely, and what you can do about each one starting today.

---

## First, a science fact you probably didn't know

Gel polish and your natural nail plate are two completely different materials. When exposed to temperature changes and moisture, they expand and contract at different rates.

Every time you go from a hot outdoor environment into air conditioning, or from a warm bath back into cool air, your natural nail plate flexes slightly — but the gel layer doesn't flex at the same rate. Over time, this microscopic mismatch creates stress at the adhesion interface, and the gel begins to separate from the nail plate at the edges.

This is exactly why gel nails tend to lift faster in summer: constant switching between heat and air conditioning puts repeated physical stress on that adhesion layer.

---

## Reason #1: Soaking Your Hands in Water

Water is one of gel's biggest enemies. Your natural nail plate absorbs water and temporarily expands — but the gel layer doesn't. That expansion gap pulls directly at the adhesion interface.

**Common culprits:**
- Washing dishes without gloves (10–20 minutes of warm water exposure)
- Long baths or hot tub soaks
- Swimming (chlorine additionally degrades the gel surface)

**Two actions you can take:**
1. Wear thick rubber dishwashing gloves whenever washing dishes or doing extended cleaning — not thin disposable gloves, but proper thick ones that keep water out
2. After soaking in a bath, while your nails are still warm, apply a drop of cuticle oil around each nail to restore the oil barrier before it dries out

---

## Reason #2: Using Your Nails as Tools

Prying open a can tab, peeling off a sticker, pressing down a sunken button — these feel minor, but they're applying a wedge-force right at the gel-to-nail interface.

Each individual incident might not cause visible damage, but do this a few times a day over two weeks and you're accumulating micro-separations along the edge.

**Two actions you can take:**
1. Keep a coin or bottle opener handy and build the reflex of reaching for a tool instead of your fingertip
2. Type with the pads of your fingers rather than your nail tips to reduce lateral pressure on the free edge

---

## Reason #3: Neglected Cuticle Care

Dry, lifted cuticles aren't just a cosmetic problem — they create a gap at the nail base where water, soap, and cleaning products can seep under the gel, breaking down the adhesive bond from the inside.

Cuticle oil isn't just about making your nails look pretty. Its core job is to **seal the cuticle** and prevent moisture from infiltrating the gel interface.

**Two actions you can take:**
1. One drop of cuticle oil per nail, every night before bed — massage it in and let it absorb (formulas with jojoba oil or sweet almond oil work best)
2. When booking your next appointment, ask your nail tech to spend a little extra time on cuticle preparation — a cleaner base = better adhesion from day one

## NG vs OK: Quick Habit Comparison

| What accelerates lifting | What extends wear |
|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Dishwashing without gloves | Thick rubber gloves every time |
| Using nails to pry things open | Use a coin or tool instead |
| No cuticle oil after soaking | Apply cuticle oil while nails are warm |
| Letting cuticles dry out | Nightly cuticle oil routine |
| Peeling off lifted gel yourself | Book a fill appointment |

---

## Gel nails are a two-way commitment

Your nail tech builds the foundation. Your daily habits determine how long it lasts.

Three principles to remember: **minimize soaking, don't use your nails as tools, and do your cuticle oil routine every night.** Follow these consistently and most gel manicures will easily reach the 3–4 week mark without significant lifting.

And if your gel is already lifting — **don't peel it off yourself.** Peeling takes layers of your natural nail plate with it. Book a fill and let your technician fix it properly.
