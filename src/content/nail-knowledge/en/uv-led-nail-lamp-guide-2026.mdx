## What Does a Nail Lamp Actually Do?

A nail lamp cures (hardens) gel polish by emitting specific wavelengths of UV or LED light that activate photoinitiators in the gel formula. Without proper curing, gel remains tacky, soft, and prone to smudging or lifting.

**Source:** Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology, 2025 — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/14732130

## UV vs LED: Key Differences

| Feature | UV Lamp | LED Lamp |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Wavelength | 320–400 nm (full UV-A) | 385–405 nm (UV-A only, narrow band) |
| Cure time | 60–120 seconds | 10–30 seconds |
| Bulb lifespan | 50–100 hours | 5,000–50,000 hours |
| Gel compatibility | Cures ALL gel types | Cures only LED-compatible gels |
| Heat spike risk | Lower | Can be higher (burn risk if overexposed) |
| Price | $10–$50 | $20–$80 |
| Mercury content | Contains mercury | No mercury |

## Can LED Lamps Cure All Gel?

**No.** Many gel polishes — especially cheaper brands and certain colors (whites, reds, dark blues) — use photoinitiators that respond to 365nm UV wavelength, which many LED lamps don't emit. Always check if your gel is "LED-compatible" or "UV/LED."

The general rule in 2026:
- Clear gels, top coats → LED cure OK
- White, neon, deep red gel polishes → often need UV or dual UV/LED lamp
- Builder/hard gels → UV preferred or high-wattage LED (48W+)

**Source:** CND (Creative Nail Design) Technical Bulletin, 2026 — https://www.cnd.com/technicians/lamp-guide

## Wattage Matters More Than Lamp Type

In 2026, the market has converged on higher wattage:

| Wattage | Best For | Cure Time |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| 6–9W | Travel/emergency only | 60–120 sec |
| 12–18W | Home use, thin layers | 30–60 sec |
| 24–36W | Standard home/salon | 15–30 sec |
| 48W+ | Professional, builder gel, fast service | 5–15 sec |

**Key finding:** A 48W LED lamp cures faster and more evenly than a 9W UV lamp. Many professional salons in 2026 have upgraded to 48W–72W LED lamps.

## UV Safety: Should You Worry?

The UV exposure from nail lamps is significantly lower than tanning beds, but repeated exposure over years has raised concerns:

- A single gel manicure = ~5–20 minutes of UV-A exposure
- Dermatologists recommend: Apply SPF 30+ to hands before UV lamp use, or use fingerless UV gloves
- No confirmed link to skin cancer from nail lamps per FDA 2025 statement, but precautionary advice is standard

**Source:** FDA Consumer Update, 2025 — https://www.fda.gov/consumers/consumer-updates/tattoos-piercings/nail-lamp-safety

## How to Use a Nail Lamp Correctly

1. **Apply gel in thin layers** — thick layers don't cure evenly
2. **Cure each layer fully** before applying next (check product instructions)
3. **Cap the free edge** — prevents chipping at the tip
4. **Don't rush** — pulling fingers out early causes undercure
5. **Wipe sticky residue** with 91%+ isopropyl alcohol after final cure

---

## Signs Your Lamp Is Under-Curing

- Gel remains tacky after full cure time
- Color can be wiped off with a cotton pad
- Gel lifts within 2–3 days
- Nails feel sticky or soft

If this happens: check lamp wattage, replace bulbs (UV), increase cure time, or switch to a higher-wattage lamp.
