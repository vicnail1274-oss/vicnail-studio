## The Nail Care Myth Problem

There's a lot of bad advice circulating about gel nail care — on social media, in nail forums, and through word-of-mouth. Much of it seems logical on the surface, which is why it spreads. But some of these beliefs actively lead people to make decisions that damage their nails.

Here are the seven most common gel nail care myths, and what's actually true.

---

## Myth 1: "Gel Nails Always Damage Your Nails"

**Reality: Gel itself doesn't damage nails. Removal technique does.**

If your nails have thinned or weakened from gel manicures, the cause is almost always improper removal — peeling, aggressive filing, or forcing the gel off rather than soaking it properly.

Correct gel removal (soaking in acetone for 15 minutes, then gently pushing off the softened gel) causes minimal damage to the nail plate. With proper aftercare afterward, nails fully recover between sets.

**Bottom line:** Find a nail technician who takes removal seriously. That matters more than avoiding gel entirely.

---

## Myth 2: "Drinking Collagen Will Strengthen Your Nails"

**Reality: It helps indirectly, but not in the way people think.**

Nails are made of keratin, not collagen. Oral collagen is broken down into amino acids during digestion and reassembled by the body as needed — it doesn't go directly to your nails and become keratin.

The nutrients with the most direct evidence for nail strength are: **biotin (B7), zinc, iron, and adequate total protein intake.** If you have brittle nail issues, address these first — the effect will be more noticeable than collagen supplements.

**Bottom line:** Collagen benefits skin and joints. For nails specifically, biotin and zinc are more targeted.

---

## Myth 3: "Cuticle Oil Is Only for Damaged Nails"

**Reality: Cuticle oil is maintenance, not emergency care.**

Many people only reach for cuticle oil when their nails are already in poor condition — like changing your car's oil only after the engine warning light appears. By then, you're managing damage rather than preventing it.

Cuticle oil keeps the skin around the nail plate flexible, prevents micro-tears from becoming the starting point of lifting, and supports the moisture balance that keeps the gel-to-nail bond strong. Daily consistent use is preventive care.

**Bottom line:** Apply cuticle oil morning and night, every day — regardless of how your nails look.

---

## Myth 4: "Getting Gel Nails Wet Will Ruin Them"

**Reality: Brief water exposure is fine. Prolonged exposure with chemicals is the problem.**

Gel nails are waterproof — that's actually one of the main reasons they last longer than regular polish. Handwashing, showering, and ordinary water contact don't meaningfully affect them.

The actual problem scenarios are: **extended soaking combined with chemical exposure.** Washing dishes in hot water with detergent, swimming in chlorinated pools, and long baths — these are the contexts where gel edge sealing gradually breaks down.

**Bottom line:** Don't avoid water. But wearing gloves for dishes is a worthwhile habit.

---

## Myth 5: "White Nails Mean Healthy Nails"

**Reality: Nail color is influenced by multiple factors — color alone doesn't indicate health.**

Some signs worth knowing about:

- **Yellow nails:** Possible fungal infection (onychomycosis), or pigment staining from using dark polish without a base coat
- **Horizontal ridges (Beau's lines):** Can indicate the body experienced significant stress — illness, high fever, surgery
- **Small pits on the nail surface:** Can be associated with psoriasis
- **Diffuse white opacity across the whole nail:** Sometimes a sign of low blood protein levels

**Bottom line:** Specific nail color changes can be informative, but need to be interpreted alongside overall health. Consult a dermatologist if something looks unusual.

---

## Myth 6: "Removing More Cuticle Before Gel = Better Adhesion"

**Reality: Over-cutting cuticle increases infection risk and doesn't improve gel longevity.**

The cuticle's job is to seal the nail root from external bacteria and moisture. Over-cutting it — especially cutting into living skin — removes this protective barrier and increases the risk of paronychia (nail fold infection).

Proper cuticle prep means: soften, gently push back, remove excess dead skin. Not complete removal.

**Bottom line:** Gel longevity comes from proper nail plate prep (dehydration, light filing, correct base coat) — not from how much cuticle you remove.

---

## Myth 7: "Influencer-Recommended Brands Are Best"

**Reality: Influencer recommendations often reflect sponsorships, not independent testing.**

There are real quality differences between gel brands, but social media endorsements frequently involve paid partnerships rather than honest evaluation.

More useful markers of quality:

- **No MMA (methyl methacrylate):** This ingredient is banned in many countries for causing severe nail damage
- **FDA or CE certification**
- **What your nail technician uses:** They apply these products daily and have genuine performance knowledge

**Bottom line:** Trust a knowledgeable nail technician over brand marketing.

---

## Getting the Foundation Right

These seven myths represent the most common — but nail care misinformation is widespread. The most effective approach is building a relationship with a nail technician you trust and asking them questions directly, rather than relying on social media or forums.

Gel nail care isn't complicated. But it has to be built on accurate information.
