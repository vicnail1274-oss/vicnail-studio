## Why Does the Same Color Look Different on Different People?

You saw a gorgeous latte-pink on Instagram, booked an appointment, and walked out wondering why it looked so flat on your hand. The color wasn't wrong — the pairing was. Gel nail colors interact with your skin tone in real-time, and understanding that relationship is the single most important factor in nail color selection.

## Step 1: Identify Your Undertone

Skin undertones fall into three categories:

- **Cool (pink or bluish)**: The veins on your inner wrist appear purple-blue
- **Warm (yellow or olive)**: Your veins appear greenish
- **Neutral**: Somewhere in between — you can wear most colors well

## Warm/Yellow Undertones: What to Avoid and What to Embrace

Warm-toned skin tends to look washed out next to similarly warm colors. The contrast is what creates a flattering effect.

**Use caution with**:
- Beige, vanilla, and cream (too close to skin color, makes hands look dull)
- Orange-brown tones (warm-on-warm can look muddy)
- Yellow-green (amplifies the yellow in your skin)

**Colors that make warm skin glow**:
- Cool pinks (rose pink, berry) — the contrast with warm skin creates a brightening effect
- Deep burgundy and wine reds — saturated enough to create impact
- Mint green and soft blue — cool tones make warm skin look more luminous
- Coral (the pink-orange middle ground that works for warm tones)

## Cool/Pink Undertones: You Have the Most Flexibility

Cool-toned skin tends to be the most forgiving when it comes to nail color. Almost everything works.

**Go ahead and try**:
- Nude pinks and warm beiges — on cool skin, warm tones look unexpectedly elegant
- Earth tones (cocoa, caramel) — won't turn yellow, reads as healthy
- Navy and royal blue — cool-on-cool for a clean, polished look

**Watch out for**:
- Very pale colors on very fair skin → your nail and hand can blend together; add some saturation to ensure the color shows up

## Deep/Tanned Skin Tones: The Best Canvas for Bold Color

Deeper skin tones have a natural contrast that makes saturated colors pop.

**Best choices**:
- Rich earth tones: terracotta, brick red — complement the skin while adding depth
- Bright saturated colors: orange-red, coral, golden yellow — the contrast creates energy
- Pure white — the contrast between deep skin and crisp white is unexpectedly chic
- Neon shades — deeper skin grounds fluorescent colors so they don't look garish

**Colors to reconsider**:
- Shades too close to your skin tone (medium-dark browns) — they can visually disappear
- Very light pinks — can look off unless they have shimmer or iridescence

## Universal Colors: Works on Everyone

When in doubt, these shades are reliably flattering across undertones:

1. **Burgundy** — arguably the most universally flattering deep color in existence
2. **Dusty Rose** — the gray undertone removes sweetness and adds sophistication
3. **Matte brick red** — warm but grounded, season-spanning
4. **Classic French (sheer pink base + white tips)** — transcends skin tone entirely

## Quick In-Salon Tips

1. Hold the color card on the back of your hand — look for harmony, not just the color itself
2. Warm skin → select a cooler version of your chosen color family (warm latte → cool pink-latte)
3. Limit yourself to 3 options max — too many choices leads to paralysis
4. Special occasions (weddings, job interviews) → choose neutral saturation, lower intensity colors for safe, elegant results

Learning to select colors for your specific skin tone takes a little practice, but once you understand the undertone principle, you'll eliminate the "something feels wrong" colors immediately — and start finding your personal sweet spot.
