## Introduction

Few things are more frustrating than spending time and money on a gel manicure, only to have the edges start lifting within a few days. Beyond the cosmetic issue, lifting gel can snag on fabric, cause nail breaks, or trap moisture and bacteria underneath. The good news: lifting is almost always preventable. This guide covers the five most common causes of gel nail lifting and how to fix or avoid each one.

---

## 5 Common Causes of Gel Nail Lifting

### Cause 1: Improper Nail Prep

The number one reason gel lifts prematurely is inadequate nail preparation. Gel bonds to a surface that is completely dry, oil-free, and free of dead skin. If the nail plate has any of the following before application, adhesion will be compromised:

- Natural skin oils (always present on the nail surface)
- Lotion or cuticle oil residue
- Lifted or unremoved dead skin at the nail edges

Lifting typically starts at the cuticle area or sides and works its way inward.

**Fix**: Before gel application, the nail technician should use alcohol or a prep pad to thoroughly degrease the nail. Cuticle skin should be fully pushed back and removed, and the nail surface lightly buffed for better adhesion.

### Cause 2: Incomplete Curing

Every layer of gel — base coat, color, and top coat — must be fully cured under the UV or LED lamp for the specified time. Common curing failures include:

- Under-powered or aging lamps (bulbs lose effectiveness over time)
- Rushing the cure time
- Applying gel too thick, preventing light from penetrating evenly through the layer

If the base coat isn't fully cured, subsequent layers won't bond properly, and lifting is inevitable.

**Fix**: Use lamps that match the gel brand's recommended wattage. Apply gel in thin, even layers and cure each one fully before moving on.

### Cause 3: No Edge Sealing (Free Edge Capping)

"Capping the free edge" means painting a thin layer of gel across the very tip of the nail — the exposed edge — so the gel wraps from the surface to the underside of the tip. This creates a seal that prevents water and impact from getting under the gel.

Without edge sealing, there's an open gap at the tip where water, detergent, and daily friction can work their way under the gel and cause it to lift from the edges inward.

**Fix**: Ask your nail technician to cap the free edge with every layer: base coat, color, and top coat.

### Cause 4: Water Exposure Too Soon After Application

Although gel nails harden immediately under the lamp, full curing and maximum adhesion take approximately 24 hours. During this window, exposure to:

- Extended water contact (washing dishes, baths, swimming)
- Harsh alkaline detergents (dish soap, laundry detergent)

...can cause the gel to soften and begin lifting from the edges.

**Fix**: Avoid prolonged water contact for the first 24 hours after getting gel nails. Wear rubber gloves when washing dishes.

### Cause 5: Shape or Length Doesn't Match Your Lifestyle

Nail shape and length affect durability in daily use:

- **Square shapes** have sharp corners that catch on surfaces and lift from the sides
- **Very long nails** have more leverage points and break more easily under pressure
- **Hands-on lifestyles** (cooking, sports, crafts) create more impact and friction than gel nails are designed for

**Fix**: Discuss your lifestyle with your nail technician and choose a shape and length that balances aesthetics with durability. Shorter, rounded shapes like oval or squoval tend to last longer.

## Emergency Fixes for Lifting Gel

### Minor Lifting (Small Edge Lifting)

If only a small corner is lifting and the rest of the nail is still well-adhered:

1. Clean the lifted area with an alcohol pad and let it dry completely.
2. If you have nail glue, apply a tiny drop under the lifted edge and press gently — this is a temporary fix to prevent further lifting until you can get it properly repaired.
3. Do not pull or peel the lifted section, as this will damage the natural nail.

### Major Lifting (Large Area or Full Lift)

If the gel is lifting over a large area or the nail is nearly detached, the safest option is to remove it properly (using the acetone soak-off method) rather than trying to patch it. Leaving severely lifted gel risks trapping bacteria and causing further nail damage when it snags.

### Pain, Redness, or Swelling

If you notice pain, redness, or swelling after gel lifting, this may indicate a bacterial or fungal issue developing under the nail. Have the gel removed professionally and consult a doctor if the symptoms persist.

## How to Prevent Lifting Long-Term

Prevention is far easier than repair. Build these habits:

1. **Daily cuticle oil**: Keeping the nail area moisturized reduces dead skin buildup at the edges, which is a primary entry point for lifting.
2. **Gloves for wet work**: Rubber gloves while washing dishes are the single most effective way to protect your gel nails.
3. **Avoid using nails as tools**: Opening cans, peeling tape, or prying things with your fingernails dramatically increases the risk of lifting and breakage. Use your fingertips instead.
4. **Stick to a 3–4 week fill cycle**: Don't let gel wear down to the natural nail before getting a fill. Regular fills are cheaper and gentler on your nails than full removal and redo.
