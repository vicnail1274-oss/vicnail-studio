Most people assume gel nails that don't last are due to low-quality gel or naturally oily nail beds. In reality, over 80% of lifting and peeling issues trace back to two steps: nail preparation and base coat application. Get these right, and four-week wear becomes the norm.

## Why Prep Matters More Than the Gel Itself

For gel to bond to the nail, it needs a clean, dry, and slightly textured surface. Natural nail oils, residual hand cream, even airborne moisture — all of these compromise adhesion. Prep is the process of removing every one of those interference factors.

### Oil Is Gel's Biggest Enemy

Nail beds naturally produce a thin layer of oil. Add daily contact with skincare products, dish soap, and cooking oils, and your nail surface is far oilier than it appears. Gel applied over oil behaves like tape applied to wax paper — it looks attached, but peels off with any lateral force.

## Correct Prep Steps

### Step 1: Push Back Cuticles

Use a cuticle pusher to gently push the cuticle back, maximizing the exposed nail surface. Cuticle remnants left on the nail plate are a common cause of lifting — gel bonded to cuticle rather than nail plate has dramatically weaker adhesion.

**Note**: You don't need to cut cuticles — just push them back.

### Step 2: Lightly Buff the Nail Surface

Use a 180–240 grit buffer to create microscopic texture on the nail plate, giving the gel material something to grip.

- Use light pressure — aggressive buffing thins the natural nail
- Buff the entire surface evenly, especially the edges and free edge
- Use a nail brush to remove dust — don't blow on it (breath moisture disrupts adhesion)

### Step 3: Degrease (The Most Critical Step)

Wipe each nail with a lint-free cotton pad soaked in isopropyl alcohol (90%+) or professional nail cleanser, wiping from the base toward the tip.

- **Wipe in one direction only** — back-and-forth wiping redistributes oil
- Use a fresh cotton pad for each nail to avoid cross-contamination
- **Do not touch the nail surface after cleaning** — even a brief contact transfers finger oils back onto the plate

### Step 4: Dehydrate (Optional but Recommended)

Professional salons often apply a dedicated nail dehydrator to remove residual moisture after the alcohol step, especially useful for clients prone to early lifting at the tip. If your gel consistently lifts at the free edge, add this step.

## Base Coat: Selection and Application

The base coat is the bridge between gel and natural nail — and the single product with the most impact on longevity.

### Three Types of Base Coat

**1. Acid Primer**
- Contains a small amount of phosphoric acid to micro-etch the nail surface, dramatically improving adhesion
- Best for: Oily nail types, nails that are notoriously difficult to bond
- Caution: Caustic — must not contact skin

**2. Non-Acid Primer**
- Gentler formulation that builds adhesion through chemical bonding
- Best for: Normal nail types, sensitive nail clients

**3. Gel Base Coat**
- The most common option — a thin layer of UV/LED-cured gel
- Many formulations double as nail-strengthening treatments

### Critical Application Details

**Thickness**: Apply thin — very thin. More base coat does not mean better adhesion. Thick base coat layers interfere with curing, leaving a soft, partially uncured layer underneath hardened gel. This creates exactly the "sandwich layer" that causes lifting.

**Cap the free edge**: After coating the nail plate, run the brush lightly along the tip of the nail, encapsulating the free edge with a thin layer of base coat. This single action significantly reduces tip lifting.

**Cure time**: Follow the manufacturer's specified curing time exactly. Under-curing is one of the most common causes of poor longevity.

## Other Factors That Affect Wear

### Daily Habits

- **Hand cream timing**: Applying lotion immediately after gel application can penetrate freshly cured edges
- **Gloves for cleaning**: Washing dishes and using household cleaners should always be done with rubber gloves
- **Stop using nails as tools**: Prying open lids is the single most common cause of tip loss in everyday life

### Nail Condition Matters

- **Thin natural nails**: Flexible nails flex more with movement, putting stress on the gel bond
- **Nail-biting history**: Nails with bitten free edges are structurally challenging for gel adhesion
- **Medication effects**: Some medications affect nail oil production, indirectly impacting adhesion

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| Tip lifting | No free edge capping / cleaning products | Cap the free edge; wear gloves for dishes |
| Entire plate peeling | Insufficient degreasing | Review cotton pad technique; use fresh pads per nail |
| Lifting at the base | Cuticle not fully pushed back / base coat too close to cuticle | Push back cuticles more thoroughly; leave a small gap from the cuticle line |
| Bubbles in gel | Base coat too thick / under-cured | Apply thinner coats; extend cure time |

## Bottom Line

Long-lasting gel comes down to three fundamentals: **a clean nail surface + correct base coat application + consistent daily care**. Get these three right and "my gel peeled at two weeks" becomes a problem of the past. A few extra minutes invested in prep translates to at least 50% longer wear — every time.
