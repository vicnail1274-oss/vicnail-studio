## Introduction

Gel manicures have become a staple beauty service for millions of people — but even regular clients often carry unanswered questions about safety, longevity, removal, and best practices. This guide compiles the 30 most frequently asked questions from VIC NAIL Studio clients, covering everything from absolute basics to nuanced health considerations.

## Chapter 1: The Basics

### Q1: What's the difference between gel nail polish and regular nail polish?

Gel nail polish is a polymer-based formula that hardens when exposed to UV or LED light in a process called polymerization. Regular polish relies on solvent evaporation to dry.

| | Gel Nails | Regular Polish |
|---|---|---|
| Longevity | 2–4 weeks | 3–7 days |
| Shine | High, chip-resistant | Moderate, fades with wear |
| Drying method | UV/LED lamp cure | Air dry |
| Removal | Acetone soak required | Standard remover |
| Thickness | Light to moderate | Thin |

### Q2: How long do gel nails actually last?

With proper application and aftercare, gel nails typically last **2 to 4 weeks**. Key factors affecting longevity:
- Quality of nail prep before application
- Daily habits (water exposure, use of harsh chemicals)
- Brand and formula quality
- Natural nail growth rate (fast growers often notice a visible gap by week 3)

### Q3: How long after getting gel nails can I get them wet?

Properly cured gel can handle normal water contact immediately. However, we recommend avoiding prolonged hot water soaking for the first **12 hours** after your appointment — this allows the gel to fully bond with the nail surface. After that, normal showering, handwashing, and swimming are fine (though extended hot baths should still be limited).

### Q4: What's the difference between UV lamps and LED lamps?

Both cure gel, but they work differently:
- **UV lamps** cure most gel formulas (including older ones), but require 2–3 minutes per coat and the bulbs need periodic replacement
- **LED lamps** cure faster (30–60 seconds per coat), are more energy-efficient, and don't require bulb replacement — but they only work with formulas designed for LED wavelengths

Most modern salons use LED lamps. If you're doing nails at home, confirm your gel brand is compatible with your lamp type.

## Chapter 2: Preparing for Your Appointment

### Q5: How should I prepare my nails before getting gel nails done?

The most impactful pre-appointment steps:
1. **Trim and shape** your nails to roughly your desired length beforehand
2. **Remove any existing polish** and wash your hands thoroughly
3. **Start softening your cuticles** with cuticle remover 1–2 days before
4. **Save reference images** — show your technician what you want rather than trying to describe it
5. **Skip lotion** on appointment day — oils and lotions on the nail surface reduce adhesion

### Q6: Can I get gel nails if my nails are very short?

Absolutely. Nail length is not a prerequisite for gel nails. Shorter nails are actually excellent candidates for gel applications — the gel can reinforce and protect them. Short nails look beautiful with solid colors, gradients, and delicate nail art designs. Just let your technician know you have short nails so they can suggest appropriate styles.

### Q7: My nails have ridges and uneven surfaces. Can I still get gel nails?

Yes. A skilled technician can address surface irregularities using ridge-filling base coats or bonding gels that smooth out the nail plate. Severe nail conditions — such as nail separation (onycholysis) or fungal infection (onychomycosis) — should be evaluated and treated by a dermatologist before proceeding with any nail services.

### Q8: How often should I get gel nails done?

The standard recommendation is every **3 to 4 weeks**. If your nails grow quickly, you may prefer "fill appointments" at 3 weeks — where only the new growth at the base is filled in — rather than a full removal and redo each time. This is faster, less expensive, and gentler on the nail.

## Chapter 3: During the Application

### Q9: Does getting gel nails hurt?

A properly done gel application should be entirely pain-free. However, you might experience:
- **Warmth or mild heat**: caused by the curing reaction, especially if layers are applied too thick or the lamp wattage is very high — tell your technician immediately if this occurs
- **Slight pressure**: during filing, which is normal
- **Stinging or irritation**: possibly indicating a sensitivity reaction to a product ingredient

Always speak up if you feel discomfort. A good technician will adjust their technique.

### Q10: What does the gel nail application process involve?

Standard gel application steps:
1. Sanitize hands and nails
2. Trim and shape the free edge
3. Push back cuticles (gently)
4. Lightly buff the nail surface for adhesion
5. Dehydrate and remove surface oils
6. Apply base coat → cure under lamp
7. Apply color gel in 2–3 thin layers, curing between each
8. Apply nail art or accents (if applicable)
9. Apply top coat → cure
10. Wipe away the inhibition layer, apply cuticle oil

### Q11: What's a "fill" appointment and how is it different from a full redo?

A **fill** (or infill) involves applying fresh gel only to the new growth at the nail base, leaving the original gel intact where it's still bonded well. This takes less time and is less expensive than a full redo. Fills work best when done 2–3 weeks after the original appointment and when the existing gel is largely undamaged.

A **full redo** means completely removing the old gel and starting fresh. This is appropriate when gel has lifted significantly, when you want a style change, or when it's been more than 4 weeks since the last application.

## Chapter 4: Health and Safety

### Q12: Do gel nails damage the natural nail?

Done correctly, gel nails don't damage the natural nail. Damage almost always comes from **improper removal** — particularly peeling or picking off gel, which strips away layers of the nail plate. Proper preparation, application, and removal leave the nail intact.

### Q13: Is UV lamp exposure safe? Does it increase skin cancer risk?

The UV exposure from gel nail lamps is real but minimal. Each coat requires only 30 seconds to 2 minutes under the lamp. Studies suggest the cumulative UV dose is far below levels associated with significant cancer risk. If you're still concerned:
- Choose LED lamps (different wavelength, lower skin penetration)
- Apply broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30+) to your hands before the appointment, avoiding the nail surface
- Use fingerless UV-blocking gloves with the fingertips cut off

### Q14: I've had nail product allergies before. Can I still get gel nails?

Prior nail product allergies — typically presenting as redness, swelling, itching, or peeling around the nails — indicate you may be sensitive to specific ingredients, most commonly HEMA (hydroxyethyl methacrylate) or other acrylates. Options include:
- Patch testing before a full application
- Requesting HEMA-free gel formulas, which are increasingly available
- Informing your technician in detail about your allergy history
- Avoiding nail services during active skin reactions

### Q15: Are gel nails safe during pregnancy?

No definitive medical evidence shows gel manicures are harmful to pregnant women or developing babies. Standard precautions:
- Ensure adequate ventilation in the salon
- Choose products free of formaldehyde, toluene, and dibutyl phthalate (the classic "3-free" minimum standard)
- Inform your technician about your pregnancy
- Consult your OB-GYN, especially during the first trimester when many recommend erring on the side of caution

### Q16: Will gel nails make my nails thinner over time?

Properly applied and removed gel nails will not thin the natural nail. Thinning occurs from:
- Overly aggressive filing during preparation
- Forcible peeling or picking at the gel
- Using low-quality or overly harsh gel products
- Getting gel nails continuously for extended periods without breaks

A reasonable practice: take a 2–4 week break from gel every 3–4 applications.

## Chapter 5: Aftercare and Maintenance

### Q17: What should I do to take care of my gel nails?

The four-habit framework for gel nail longevity:
1. **Use your fingertip pads, not your nail tips** — for typing, opening things, peeling tape
2. **Wear rubber gloves for wet chores** — dishes, cleaning, chemical exposure
3. **Apply cuticle oil daily** — massage into the cuticle area before bed
4. **Trim, don't peel** — if lifting occurs, trim the lifted edge with scissors and book a repair

### Q18: My gel is lifting at the edges. What should I do?

- **Small edge lift**: trim the lifted portion with small scissors to prevent it spreading further, and schedule a fill or repair soon
- **Large area lifting or peeling**: a full removal and redo is likely necessary
- **Never force-peel**: this removes natural nail layers and causes significant damage that takes weeks to heal

### Q19: I see green discoloration under my gel nail. What is it?

Green discoloration (sometimes called "green nail syndrome") is caused by *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* bacteria thriving in moisture trapped between the gel and nail. Remove the gel immediately, clean with isopropyl alcohol, and consult a dermatologist if the discoloration is extensive or spreading. Do not apply new gel until the nail has fully healed.

### Q20: Can I paint regular nail polish over my gel nails?

Yes — but use acetone-free nail polish remover to take it off. Acetone-based remover will degrade the gel top coat. Use good-quality polish; cheaper polishes often contain strong solvents that can cloud the gel top coat over time even if they're not primarily acetone.

## Chapter 6: Removal

### Q21: How do I properly remove gel nails at home?

Safe home removal process:
1. Buff the top coat with a 180–240 grit file until the shine is gone
2. Soak cotton pads with pure acetone and press onto each nail
3. Wrap each finger with aluminum foil to hold the cotton and prevent evaporation
4. Wait 15–20 minutes
5. Gently push off softened gel with an orange wood stick, working from base to tip
6. Rewrap any stubborn spots for 5–10 more minutes — never scrape hard
7. Buff the nail surface smooth, apply cuticle oil generously

### Q22: Can I just peel off gel nails?

**No.** Peeling gel nails removes the gel along with the surface layers of the natural nail plate — the keratin layers that give the nail its strength and smoothness. The result is thin, fragile, rough nails that need months to recover. Acetone soak-off is the only way to remove gel safely.

### Q23: Will acetone harm my skin? How do I protect it?

Acetone is very drying to skin. To protect the skin around your nails:
- Apply a thick layer of petroleum jelly (Vaseline) to the skin around each nail before starting — it acts as a barrier
- After removal, wash your hands with soap and apply rich hand cream and cuticle oil immediately

### Q24: My nails feel soft and weak after gel removal. Is this normal?

Yes, temporary nail softness after gel removal is common and expected. Recovery strategies:
- Apply a nail strengthening treatment daily for 2–3 weeks
- Use cuticle oil consistently to maintain flexibility
- Consider a biotin supplement (2,500–5,000 mcg daily) for improved nail protein synthesis
- Give nails at least 2–4 weeks of rest before the next gel application

## Chapter 7: Special Situations

### Q25: Can I get gel nails if I have nail fungus?

Not recommended. The warm, enclosed environment under gel is ideal for fungal growth, and applying gel over an infected nail can significantly worsen the infection. Complete antifungal treatment and confirm the nail has fully recovered before resuming gel nail services.

### Q26: After curing, I feel tingling or numbness in my fingers. Is this normal?

Mild warmth during curing is normal and harmless. Tingling that lasts longer than a few minutes, or any persistent numbness, swelling, or redness, may indicate a sensitivity or allergic reaction to a gel ingredient. Report it to your technician and seek medical advice if symptoms persist or worsen.

### Q27: Can gel nails be used to extend the length of my nails?

Yes. There are two primary methods:
- **Nail forms**: A paper or metal template is attached to the free edge, and gel is built up over it to create an extension, then the form is removed
- **Nail tips + gel**: Pre-formed plastic or soft gel tips are adhered and then encapsulated with gel

Extensions require more maintenance than standard gel manicures (fills every 2–3 weeks) and need a skilled technician with extension experience. They're excellent for occasions or for people who want length they can't grow naturally.

### Q28: Are gel nails only for women?

Not at all. Nail care has no gender. Male clients are a fast-growing segment of the nail industry, and gel nails offer the same practical and aesthetic benefits to everyone. Popular options among male clients include clear protective coats, deep solid colors (black, navy, forest green, dark brown), and subtle minimalist designs.

## Chapter 8: Choosing Your Nail Technician

### Q29: What should I look for when choosing a nail technician?

Key factors to evaluate:
1. **Hygiene practices**: Are tools properly sterilized? Are single-use files used per client?
2. **Training and credentials**: Did they complete professional nail technician training? Do they hold relevant certifications?
3. **Portfolio**: Do their Instagram or photo examples match the style quality you're looking for?
4. **Communication**: Do they ask about your preferences and lifestyle? Do they make suggestions based on your nail health?
5. **Reviews**: Look for consistent feedback about hygiene, longevity, and aftercare advice — not just aesthetics

### Q30: What does a gel manicure cost, and what affects the price?

Pricing varies significantly by location, technician skill level, and the complexity of the design. General ranges (Taiwan pricing context):
- Solid color / basic application: NT$500–900
- Intermediate designs (gradients, simple nail art): NT$800–1,500
- Complex or artistic designs: NT$1,500–3,000+
- Extensions: additional NT$300–800+ depending on method

Price factors:
- Design complexity (plain color vs. hand-painted art)
- Whether extensions are included
- Technician experience level and brand positioning
- Product quality (imported premium brands cost more)
- Location (urban centers vs. smaller cities)
