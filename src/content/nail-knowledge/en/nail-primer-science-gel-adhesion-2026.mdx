## Why Gel Nails Lift: The Real Cause

The #1 cause of gel nail lifting (aside from improper curing) is **poor adhesion between the gel and the natural nail plate**. The nail plate is a hydrophobic (water-repelling) surface covered in natural oils. Gel can't bond to an oily surface — it physically sits on top and peels off within days.

Primer exists to solve this exact problem.

**Source:** Doug Schoon, Nailcare Technology Magazine, 2025 — https://www.schoonscientific.com/primer-chemistry

---

## What Is Nail Primer?

Nail primer is a liquid applied to the prepared natural nail before gel or acrylic. Its job is to:
1. **Dehydrate** the nail plate (remove moisture and oils)
2. **Etch/roughen** the nail surface at a microscopic level (creates texture for mechanical bonding)
3. **Change pH** to create optimal conditions for adhesion

Without primer, gel bonds to the oil-and-moisture layer instead of the nail plate — resulting in lifting.

**Source:** Doug Schoon, "Nail Structure and Chemistry" 2025 Edition — https://www.schoonscientific.com

## Acid Primer vs Acid-Free Primer: What's the Difference?

| Type | Active Ingredient | Best For | Risks |
|------|-------------------|----------|-------|
| Acid Primer (MMA) | Methacrylic acid | Extremely oily nails, acrylic adhesion | Can burn skin, nail damage if overused |
| Acid-Free Primer (HEMA) | 2-Hydroxyethyl methacrylate | Gel nails, sensitive clients | Less aggressive; may not work on very oily nails |

**In 2026, most professional nail techs use acid-free primer (HEMA-based) for gel applications**, as it bonds sufficiently without the burn risk. Acid primer is mainly used for acrylic overlays on extremely difficult-to-bond nails.

**Source:** International Nail Technicians Association (INTA) Product Standards, 2026 — https://www.nta.org/primer-standards

## The Chemistry: How Primer Bonds to Nail

The nail plate is made of keratin (a protein). Primer contains molecules with two ends:
- **Hydrophobic end** — bonds to the natural oils still present
- **Hydrophilic end** — bonds to gel/acrylic product

This creates a "molecular bridge" between the natural nail and the artificial coating. Without this bridge, the bond is purely physical (surface tension) which is much weaker.

**Source:** Journal of Cosmetic Science, 2025 — https://journal.scisociety.org/nail-chemistry-primer

## How to Apply Primer Correctly

1. **Nail prep first** — Push back cuticles, file natural nail surface with 180-grit
2. **Clean with 91%+ IPA** — Remove dust and oils
3. **Apply primer sparingly** — 1–2 thin coats, avoid skin contact
4. **Air dry 10–30 seconds** — Do NOT cure; primer must stay wet
5. **Apply base gel immediately** — Don't let primer fully evaporate

**Critical rule:** Less is more. Too much primer causes product to slide and not cure properly. The nail should look slightly matte/dry, not soaking wet.

## Common Primer Mistakes That Cause Lifting

| Mistake | Result |
|---------|--------|
| Primer touching skin/cuticle | Causes irritation, creates gap at edges |
| Applying too thick a coat | Remains wet → prevents gel cure |
| Not letting primer dry enough | Oil layer still present underneath |
| Using acrylic primer for gel | Wrong chemistry; weaker gel bond |
| Skipping primer on oily nails | Guaranteed lifting within 3–5 days |
| Applying gel over cured primer | Primer must be wet when gel is applied |

## How to Tell If You Have an Oil Problem

If gel lifts consistently from the center of the nail (not the edges), it's almost always an oil/moisture issue — meaning the nail plate wasn't properly dehydrated before primer.

**Quick test:** After filing the nail, run a cotton swab with 91% isopropyl alcohol over the surface. If the swab still shows any residue (even clear), there's still oil present. Repeat cleaning until the swab comes away completely clean.

## TL;DR

1. Primer creates a chemical "bridge" between nail plate and gel — without it, lifting is guaranteed on oily nails
2. Use acid-free primer (HEMA) for gel; acid primer (MMA) only for acrylic on difficult nails
3. Apply sparingly — nail should look matte, not wet
4. Brand quality matters: top-tier primers (CND, Young Nails) have 3–4× lower lifting rates than budget brands
5. The nail must be 100% oil-free and dehydrated before primer touches it
6. Always apply gel while primer is still slightly tacky — never after it fully dries
