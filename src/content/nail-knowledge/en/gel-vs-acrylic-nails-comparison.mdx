"Should I get gel or acrylic?" is the most common question from first-time nail clients. Both involve adding a product layer to your nails, but the materials, application process, and ideal candidates are completely different. Here's the full breakdown.

## How They Work

### Gel Nails

Gel nails use a UV/LED-curable gel product. After application, the gel is cured under a UV or LED lamp to harden. The process has no strong chemical smell, and the finished result is lightweight and natural-looking.

### Acrylic Nails

Acrylic nails are created by mixing acrylic powder (polymer) with a liquid monomer. The mixture is sculpted onto the nail and hardens through a chemical reaction with open air — no lamp required. There is a noticeable chemical smell during application.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

### Appearance and Feel

- **Gel:** Clear, glossy finish that closely mimics natural nails. Best for the "you can't tell I have nail enhancements" look.
- **Acrylic:** Harder, more structural feel. Greater shape flexibility. Better for complex designs and dramatic length.

### Application Time

- **Gel:** Approximately 60–90 minutes including prep
- **Acrylic:** Approximately 90–120 minutes (sculpting requires more time)

### How Long They Last

- **Gel:** 2–4 weeks, then infill or removal
- **Acrylic:** 3–4 weeks, but requires infill visits every 2–3 weeks to fill the gap at the base as the natural nail grows

### Nail Damage

- **Gel:** Lower damage overall, but peeling off improperly causes significant surface damage
- **Acrylic:** Involves more filing of the natural nail surface; repeated sets over time can thin the nail plate

### Price Range (USD)

- **Gel:** Basic set ~$30–60; with nail art ~$60–120
- **Acrylic:** Basic extension ~$50–80; with sculpting and art ~$80–160

## Pros and Cons

### Gel Nails — Pros
- No strong chemical smell; comfortable application experience
- Lightweight feel, very natural appearance
- Wide color and design selection — gradients, blends, hand art all work beautifully
- Fast cure time (LED lamp: 30–60 seconds per layer)

### Gel Nails — Cons
- Limited extension length (too long = prone to snapping)
- Requires UV/LED lamp equipment
- Can pop off entirely on impact

### Acrylic Nails — Pros
- Extremely durable; ideal for dramatic length
- No lamp required; self-hardens in air
- Localized repairs possible without full removal
- Best for 3D sculpting and intricate designs

### Acrylic Nails — Cons
- Chemical odor during application
- Heavier, less natural feel
- Longer removal process required
- Higher technical skill required from the nail tech

## How to Choose: Match to Your Needs

| Your Need | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Daily wear, natural-looking finish | Gel |
| Very short nails wanting significant length | Acrylic |
| First-time nail client | Gel |
| Complex 3D or sculptural designs | Acrylic |
| Budget-conscious | Gel |
| Need maximum durability (hands-on work) | Acrylic |
| Sensitive to chemical smells | Gel |
| Want long nails to last as long as possible | Acrylic |

## Common Myths — Debunked

**Myth 1: Acrylic damages nails more than gel.**
Not entirely accurate. Both affect the nail surface. The bigger variable is how they're removed. Proper acetone soak removal causes less damage than improperly peeling gel off.

**Myth 2: Gel can't be used for extensions.**
It can — but keep the extension to within roughly one-third the length of your natural nail bed. Beyond that ratio, the risk of breakage rises significantly.

**Myth 3: Acrylics always look fake and thick.**
That's a skill issue, not a material issue. In the hands of an experienced nail tech, acrylic can look just as natural and thin as gel — sometimes more so.

---

There's no universally "better" option. Gel and acrylic each excel in different contexts. Understand what matters to you — lifestyle, look, maintenance tolerance — and choose accordingly. If you're truly undecided, starting with gel is the lower-commitment entry point. You can always experience acrylic later and compare.
