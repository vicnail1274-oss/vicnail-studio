## Why Does Your Manicure Never Last?

Spent a good amount on a fresh manicure only to see it lifting, peeling, or popping off before two weeks are up?

Most people assume it's the nail tech's fault, or the product quality. But the truth is: **what you do after you leave the salon matters more than what happens in it.**

Proper aftercare can add one to two weeks to the life of your gel or hard gel manicure. Bad habits can have it lifting within three days of walking out the door.

Here are 10 tips your nail tech wants you to know — even if she's never said them out loud.

---

## Tip 1: Avoid Prolonged Water Exposure for the First 24 Hours

The first 24 hours after a manicure is the "setting period." The gel may look fully cured, but adhesion at the edges and base is still developing.

During this window:
- Avoid long soaks (baths, extended dishwashing)
- Pat hands dry gently after washing — don't rub
- Use your fingertip pads (not nails) to scrub your scalp when washing hair

It sounds fussy for one day, but the payoff in durability is significant.

---

## Tip 2: Use Cuticle Oil Daily — It's More Important Than You Think

Cuticle oil is the most underrated nail care product out there.

Most people assume it's just a shine booster. But its real function is to **maintain moisture balance between the gel and your natural nail.** When your nails become too dry, the bond between gel and nail weakens — and that's when edges start to lift.

**How to use it:** Apply morning and night, massaging gently around the cuticle and nail fold. Every single day — not just when you remember.

Brand doesn't matter much. CND, OPI, Orly all make affordable options. Jojoba oil or olive oil work too.

---

## Tip 3: Wear Gloves for Housework

Dish soap, cleaning sprays, bleach — these are far more corrosive to gel than most people realize.

Regular exposure to household cleaners dulls the finish and causes edge lifting over time. If you do significant cleaning daily, a pair of rubber gloves is one of the easiest ways to extend your manicure life — and protect your skin while you're at it.

---

## Tip 4: Stop Using Your Nails as Tools

"Just this once." "Just this angle." Most nail damage happens in these small moments.

Prying open plastic wrap, digging at a gap in a credit card slot, popping a bottle cap — every one of these applies lateral stress to the gel. Over time, that stress causes chipping and lifting at the edges.

Build the habit of using your fingertip pads instead of nail tips. Keep a small bottle opener in your bag.

---

## Tip 5: Rehydrate After Hot Springs or Swimming

Hot spring water (sulfur) and pool water (chlorine) are particularly hard on gel. After soaking, nails often look hazy and edges start to lift.

**Post-swim rescue routine:**
1. Rinse hands thoroughly with fresh water
2. Pat dry and apply cuticle oil immediately
3. If you have topcoat on hand, brush a thin layer over the nail for extra protection

If you swim regularly, make this part of your routine every time.

---

## Tip 6: Don't Ignore Lifted Edges — Get Them Fixed

When you notice an edge starting to lift, most people think "I'll deal with it next appointment."

That's the wrong call.

A lifted edge allows moisture and air to get between the gel and natural nail, accelerating full detachment — and in some cases, extended moisture exposure can cause issues underneath. Once you notice lifting, get back to your nail tech for a patch as soon as possible. Most studios offer small repair services at low cost.

---

## Tip 7: Keep Shower Time Nail-Smart

It's not just swimming. Daily showers count too.

Hot water above roughly 40°C (104°F) sustained on nails softens the gel slightly and weakens edge adhesion. You don't need cold showers — just try to minimize the amount of time your hands are directly under hot water. Let the main stream hit your body rather than your hands.

---

## Tip 8: Schedule Regular Maintenance, Not Just Emergency Repairs

Many people think "if it hasn't fallen off, I don't need to go back." Nail techs recommend otherwise.

Even when gel looks intact after 3–4 weeks, your natural nail has grown out underneath, creating a gap at the base. That gap accumulates debris and stress. Regular check-ins let your tech trim edges, fill gaps, and keep your nails healthy — meaning the gel lasts longer and your natural nail stays in better shape.

---

## Tip 9: Proper Removal is Non-Negotiable

Most nail damage doesn't happen during application — it happens during removal.

**Correct removal process:**
1. File the top coat surface to allow acetone penetration
2. Soak cotton pads in pure acetone
3. Wrap in foil and wait 10–15 minutes
4. Gently push — let the gel come off on its own
5. If there's residue, soak again. **Never force or peel.**

One bad peel session can thin your natural nail enough that it takes 3–6 months to fully recover.

---

## Tip 10: Give Your Nails a Rest Between Sets

Continuous back-to-back gel sets can lead to yellowing and thinning over time. After every 3–4 gel sets, give yourself 1–2 weeks of bare nail time.

During the break: apply cuticle oil generously and use a nail strengthener (Nail Hardener). This restores elasticity and thickness, so your next gel set has a healthier base to bond to.

## Final Thought: Your Manicure Is an Investment — Protect It

A well-executed manicure represents hours of your nail tech's skill and your money. What you do at home determines how long that investment holds.

Nail aftercare doesn't take much time — just a few habit adjustments. Keep this guide bookmarked and follow it after your next appointment. You might be surprised how much longer your nails can last.
