Most people think nail care is just about choosing a color and booking a salon visit. But what actually keeps nails beautiful and healthy long-term is the mundane stuff: daily habits that accumulate quietly into strong, resilient nails.

This guide gives you a complete routine — from everyday micro-habits to pre- and post-application care and recovery after removal.

---

## Part 1: Daily Maintenance

### 1. Cuticle Oil — Every Night

**Frequency:** Once daily, preferably before bed

Cuticle oil is the single most impactful nail care product you can add to your routine. Nail health begins at the root — dry, peeling cuticles mean brittle, splitting nails. A small drop of cuticle oil massaged into each nail's base each night is worth more than any other treatment.

**Best ingredients:**
- Jojoba oil: small molecules, absorbs quickly without residue
- Vitamin E: antioxidant, repairs damage
- Argan oil: rich and long-lasting moisture

### 2. Hand Cream After Every Wash

**Frequency:** After each hand wash

Frequent washing strips natural oils from both skin and nails. Keep a hand cream at your sink and apply after each wash. It becomes automatic within days.

**Note:** Skip hand cream for 2–3 hours before any nail appointment — oils on the nail plate will prevent your base coat from bonding properly.

### 3. Stop Using Nails as Tools

Hard to build, but with the biggest payoff:
- Don't use nails to peel stickers or open lids
- Don't type aggressively with nail tips
- Default to using the pad of your finger, not the tip

## Part 2: Pre-Application Prep

### 1. Clean and Degrease

On the day of your manicure, avoid applying hand lotion or oil-based skincare on your hands. Right before you begin, wipe every nail with an isopropyl alcohol pad or nail dehydrator. This removes oils and ensures your base coat bonds properly to the nail plate.

### 2. Shape First

If doing your own nails, finalize your nail shape before starting any gel application.
- Always file in one direction — back-and-forth sawing causes nail layer separation
- Use a 180–240 grit file for natural nails
- Changing shape mid-application disrupts the integrity of your gel layers

### 3. Push Back Cuticles

Use a wooden cuticle pusher to gently push back cuticles before application. This allows base coat to bond closely to the nail root. Avoid cutting cuticles unless there is significant overgrowth — over-trimming increases infection risk.

---

## Part 3: During Application

### Cure Each Layer Fully

Never rush curing times for gel manicures. Every layer must fully cure before you apply the next. Undercured layers are weaker and bond poorly.

Dark or dense gels may need 10–15 extra seconds.

### Cap the Free Edge

After applying each layer, drag your brush vertically across the nail's tip (the free edge) to seal it. This is the most effective single technique for preventing early tip lifting.

### Don't Touch Uncured Gel

Keep uncured gel away from any surfaces before curing — contact leaves fingerprints and dents in the surface.

---

## Part 4: Post-Application Care

### The First 24 Hours

- Avoid prolonged water contact for at least 24 hours after application — allow the base layer to fully settle
- Skip baths, pools, and heavy dishwashing
- Hold off on heavy hand cleaning tasks

### Ongoing Daily Care

- Continue daily cuticle oil — hydrated nail beds hold gel better
- Wear rubber gloves for dishes and cleaning
- If a nail starts to lift, don't peel it — use a UV nail glue drop to temporarily seal it, or visit the salon soon

---

## Part 5: Post-Removal Recovery

After gel or acrylic removal, nails are in their most vulnerable state. This period is important.

### Week 1: Rest and Repair

- Let natural nails breathe without any gel products for 1–2 weeks
- Apply cuticle oil twice daily (morning and night)
- Use a hardening base coat with keratin or similar strengthening ingredients — a thin protective layer without color

### Week 2 Onward: Gradual Return

- If nails still feel thin or fragile, start with regular polish and a nail strengthener base coat before returning to gel
- Avoid immediately jumping back into acrylic or long soak-off removal cycles

### Strengthening Very Thin Nails

For nails that are unusually thin after removal:
- Look for silicone-containing hardening base coats
- Minimize further acetone exposure — reduce removal frequency or use gentler removal methods
- Consider biotin (vitamin B7) supplementation — research supports its role in nail strength

## Part 6: Quick FAQ

**Q: My gel manicure looks thick and unnatural. What went wrong?**
A: Too much product per layer. Apply each coat thin — multiple thin layers beat one thick layer every time.

**Q: My nails always lift early. Is it the gel brand or something I'm doing?**
A: Usually one of three things: insufficient degreasing, not capping the free edge, or under-curing. Troubleshoot in that order.

**Q: How much cuticle oil should I use?**
A: One drop per nail. More isn't better — consistency is. A drop nightly beats occasional heavy application.

**Q: My nails break constantly. Could it be a nutritional issue?**
A: Possibly. Deficiencies in biotin (B7), iron, and protein are all linked to brittle nails. If it's a persistent issue, consult a dietitian or dermatologist.

## Summary: The Three Pillars of Nail Care

1. **Daily:** Cuticle oil + hand cream after washing + no nail-as-tool habits
2. **During application:** Proper degreasing + edge sealing + full cure times
3. **After removal:** Rest period + recovery routine + gradual return

This routine takes maybe 5 minutes a day. The compounding effect over weeks and months is significant — healthy, beautiful nails aren't luck, they're maintained.
