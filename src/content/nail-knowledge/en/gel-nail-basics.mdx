## What Are Gel Nails?

Gel nails use special gel formulas that cure under UV or LED light to form a durable, glossy coating on the nail. Compared to regular nail polish, gel nails last significantly longer (2–4 weeks), maintain their shine through daily activities, and won't chip from normal wear.

Understanding the basics helps you communicate better at the salon, choose the right gel type for your needs, and get the results you're actually looking for.

## Types of Gel

### 1. Hard Gel

Hard gel cures into a rigid, very durable layer. It's primarily used for nail extensions and sculpting. Unlike soft gels, **hard gel cannot be removed by soaking in acetone** — it must be filed off, which requires a skilled technician.

**Best for:**
- Building length on very short natural nails
- High-wear situations requiring maximum durability
- As a structural base layer for sculptural designs

### 2. Soft Gel (Soak-off Gel)

The most common gel type used in salons worldwide. Soft gel has some flexibility after curing and **can be removed by soaking in acetone for 10–15 minutes**. Nearly all gel polish colors are soft gel formulas.

**Best for:**
- Regular salon manicures (solid colors, gradients, nail art)
- Clients who like to change colors frequently
- People who prioritize natural nail health

### 3. Builder Gel

Builder gel sits between hard and soft gel — it adds structural strength to the natural nail plate but can still be soaked off. It's mainly used to **thicken thin or fragile nails**, repair breaks, or create a subtle extension.

**Best for:**
- People whose nails break frequently or are unusually thin
- Creating a fuller, more sculpted shape without a full extension
- Emergency nail repair at the salon

## Essential Tools

Before starting a gel manicure, you need the following:

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| UV / LED curing lamp | Core equipment for hardening gel. LED lamps cure in 30–60 sec; UV lamps take ~120 sec but work with more gel brands |
| Base coat | Bonds gel to the natural nail and protects the nail plate |
| Color gel | Provides color — typically applied in 2–3 thin layers |
| Top coat | Seals color, adds shine; choose glossy or matte finish |
| Nail prep solution | Removes oil and moisture before application |
| Cleanser | Wipes away the uncured inhibition layer after final cure |
| Nail files / buffer | Lightly roughen the nail surface for better adhesion |
| Cuticle pusher / orange stick | Push back cuticles to prevent gel from touching skin |

## Step-by-Step Application

Following the correct sequence is what separates a long-lasting gel manicure from one that lifts in a week:

**Step 1: Nail prep**
Wash hands → push back cuticles → lightly buff nail surface to remove shine → apply nail prep solution → let it evaporate fully

**Step 2: Base coat**
Apply a thin, even layer over the entire nail plate → cure under lamp (LED: 30–60 seconds)

**Step 3: First color layer**
Apply a thin, even coat → cure fully. Thin layers prevent wrinkling.

**Step 4: Second (and third) color layer**
Build up pigment saturation → cure after each layer

**Step 5: Top coat**
Apply over the entire nail surface and **cap the free edge** (seal the nail tip) → cure → wipe away any uncured inhibition layer if needed

## Four Key Techniques for Beginners

**1. Always apply thin layers**
Thick coats wrinkle, cure unevenly, and feel heavy. Multiple thin layers create a smoother, more durable result every time.

**2. Cap the free edge**
Running your brush along the nail tip on the top coat step is one of the most important habits to develop. It seals the edge and dramatically slows down chipping at the tips.

**3. Maintain a cuticle gap**
Gel that touches the skin will lift from the skin edge as it cures, creating pockets that eventually peel back. Keep a 0.5–1mm gap from the cuticle at all times.

**4. Cure every layer fully**
Under-cured gel feels tacky, transfers to other nails, and compromises adhesion of subsequent layers. Don't rush the lamp time — the few extra seconds matter.

## After-Care Reminder

What you do after leaving the salon determines how long your gel manicure actually lasts. Apply cuticle oil daily, wear gloves when washing dishes, and avoid using your nails as tools. These simple habits can extend a two-week set to three weeks or longer.
