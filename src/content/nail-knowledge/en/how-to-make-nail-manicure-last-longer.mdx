A manicure that starts lifting or chipping within days is one of the most frustrating nail experiences. The good news: durability isn't just about your nail tech's skill — your own habits account for a huge portion of how long a manicure lasts.

These 7 habits, applied consistently, can push a gel manicure to 3–4 weeks and regular polish several days beyond the usual.

---

## Habit 1: Properly Degrease Before Application

**Why it matters:** Natural oils on the nail plate — from your skin, residual hand lotion, anything — are the #1 enemy of base coat adhesion. Oil creates a barrier that prevents proper bonding, leading to early lifting.

**How to do it:**
- Wipe every nail with a cotton pad soaked in isopropyl alcohol or a nail dehydrator immediately before applying base coat
- Skip hand lotion for a few hours before your manicure
- Don't do a hand massage before a nail session — oils seep into the nail grooves

**Common mistake:** Washing hands right before starting and jumping straight in — water and skin oils are still on the surface.

---

## Habit 2: Seal the Free Edge on Every Layer

**Why it matters:** Lifting almost always starts at the tip. When the base coat, color, and top coat all wrap around the very edge of the nail (the free edge), that starting point for lifting is eliminated.

**How to do it:**
- When applying each layer, swipe the brush vertically along the nail's cut edge to "cap" it
- This applies to every layer: base coat, color, top coat
- This single technique has more impact on longevity than almost anything else

**How to check:** After curing, look at your nail from the side — you shouldn't see a raw gel edge peeking out from the tip.

---

## Habit 3: Cure Long Enough

**Why it matters:** Under-cured gel is significantly weaker than fully cured gel — it's more prone to denting, lifting, and early damage. It also doesn't bond as well to the next layer.

**How to do it:**
- Follow the recommended cure times on your gel (typically: base coat 30s, color 60s, top coat 60s)
- Replace lamp bulbs periodically — output degrades over time, meaning old bulbs need longer cure times
- Add 10–15 extra seconds for dark or thick gel colors

**Common mistake:** Assuming the nail is cured because it feels solid on the surface — the interior layer may still be soft.

---

## Habit 4: Refresh Your Top Coat (For Regular Polish)

**Why it matters:** Regular polish top coats aren't as durable as gel. But refreshing with a new layer every 2–3 days significantly extends wear time — essentially "repairing" the protective layer before it fails.

**How to do it:**
- Once your polish is fully dry, re-apply a thin quick-dry top coat every 2–3 days
- Always cap the free edge when refreshing
- Use a quick-dry formula to minimize inconvenience

---

## Habit 5: Stop Using Your Nails as Tools

**Why it matters:** Prying open lids, scratching off labels, picking up coins — these actions put lateral stress directly on the nail tip and are the fastest way to chip or crack a manicure.

**How to do it:**
- Consciously shift to using the pad of your finger instead of the tip
- Use a coin, toothpick, or bottle opener for lids
- Use scissors instead of your nails for tape and packaging

This requires deliberate effort to change, but the longevity improvement is noticeable almost immediately.

---

## Habit 6: Wear Gloves for Chemical Exposure

**Why it matters:** Dish soap, household cleaners, bleach, and chlorine all accelerate gel lifting and strip the gloss from regular polish. Even frequent hand washing without gloves contributes.

**How to do it:**
- Wear rubber gloves for washing dishes and cleaning
- Always glove up with hair dye, cleaning sprays, and solvents
- Rinse chlorine off quickly after swimming; prolonged water soaking weakens adhesion across all nail types

**Practical tip:** Keep a pair of rubber gloves next to the sink. One second to put them on before dishes is the highest-ROI nail care investment you can make.

---

## Habit 7: Fill In on Schedule (Gel Nails)

**Why it matters:** Nails grow roughly 0.1mm per day. After 3 weeks, there's a noticeable gap at the cuticle area (the "regrowth zone"). Leaving it too long means moisture and debris can get under the gel, accelerating lifting.

**How to do it:**
- Book a gel fill-in (not a full removal and redo) every 3–4 weeks
- Fill-ins are faster, cheaper, and less damaging to natural nails than full redos
- Waiting until week 5–6 means more nail reshaping work and higher lift risk

---

## Bonus Habit: Daily Cuticle Oil

While this doesn't directly extend polish durability, applying cuticle oil each night keeps the nail plate and surrounding skin healthy — making the whole manicure look more polished and professional longer.

**Best ingredients to look for:** jojoba oil, vitamin E, argan oil.

---

## Summary

Most of what determines your manicure's longevity is in your hands — literally:

1. Degrease before application
2. Seal the free edge on every layer
3. Cure long enough
4. Refresh top coat regularly (regular polish)
5. Stop using nails as tools
6. Wear gloves for chemical exposure
7. Keep up with fill-in appointments (gel)

These seven habits together create a foundation for a manicure that works with you, not against you.
