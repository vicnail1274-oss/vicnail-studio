Before getting a gel manicure, you probably have questions: Will it damage my nails? Can I get them wet? Do I have to go to a salon to remove them? Here are answers to 30 of the most common beginner questions.

## The Basics

### 1. What is a gel manicure?

A gel manicure involves applying a gel product (a semi-liquid polymer) to your natural nails, then curing it under a UV or LED lamp. Once cured, gel is more durable and longer-lasting than regular nail polish.

### 2. How long does a gel manicure last?

Standard gel manicures last **2 to 4 weeks**. Hard gel extensions can last 4 to 6 weeks. Longevity depends on:
- Nail prep quality (removing oils and moisture)
- Gel brand and formula
- Your daily habits (cleaning products, water exposure)

### 3. What's the difference between gel nails and acrylic nails?

| Feature | Gel Nails | Acrylic Nails |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Curing method | UV/LED lamp | Air-dry (liquid + powder reaction) |
| Appearance | Natural-looking shine | Thicker, more opaque |
| Removal | Soak in acetone | File off |
| Smell | Low | Strong |

### 4. What's the difference between soft gel and hard gel?

- **Soft gel (soak-off gel)**: Removed with acetone soak, thinner, best for everyday manicures
- **Hard gel**: Must be filed off, ideal for extensions or when maximum strength is needed

## Safety and Health

### 5. Does gel nail polish damage your nails?

Done correctly, gel manicures cause minimal damage. Most damage comes from:
- Peeling or picking gel off without soaking
- Over-filing the natural nail during prep or removal
- Not giving nails occasional breaks

### 6. Do I need to give my nails a "break" from gel?

Not necessarily. With proper application and removal, continuous gel manicures are generally fine. If you notice your nails thinning, peeling, or turning white, take a 1 to 2 month break and use a nail strengthener.

### 7. Is the UV/LED lamp safe?

Modern LED lamps use low wattage with very short cure times. For typical salon use, the UV exposure is minimal and considered safe. If concerned, apply SPF to your hands or wear fingerless UV-protective gloves.

### 8. Can I get a gel manicure while pregnant?

Consult your OB-GYN first. Some gel formulas contain HEMA (hydroxyethyl methacrylate), which can cause allergic reactions. HEMA-free gel options are generally considered a safer choice during pregnancy.

### 9. What if I'm allergic to gel?

Signs of an allergic reaction include redness, itching, or swelling around the nail area. Stop immediately and see a doctor. Switch to HEMA-free or hypoallergenic formulas to reduce the risk.

## Application and Care

### 10. What should I do to prepare before getting gel nails?

Make sure your nails are clean and dry:
- Don't soak hands before your appointment (reduces adhesion)
- Remove any existing oil or residue from the nail surface
- Clean up excess cuticles

### 11. Can I get gel nails wet?

Yes. However, avoid prolonged soaking (baths, swimming) within the first **24 hours** after application to allow the gel to fully harden.

### 12. When can I get my hands wet after a gel manicure?

Brief contact with water is fine immediately after curing. For extended soaking, wait **12 to 24 hours**.

### 13. How do I make gel nails last longer?

Five habits that extend gel manicure life:
1. Wear gloves when doing dishes or cleaning
2. Avoid using your nails as tools
3. Apply cuticle oil weekly
4. Avoid prolonged contact with saltwater or harsh chemicals
5. Keep hands moisturized

### 14. What should I do if my gel nails lift?

Minor lifting at the edges can be fixed with a small amount of gel. Significant lifting should be fully removed and redone. Never peel or force the gel off — this takes layers of your natural nail with it.

### 15. Can I do gel nails at home?

Yes. You'll need a UV/LED lamp, base coat, gel polish, and top coat, plus basic nail prep tools. Watch tutorial videos before starting, especially for the prep and capping techniques.

## Removal

### 16. Do I have to go to a salon to remove gel nails?

No. Soft gel nails can safely be removed at home. You'll need acetone, cotton pads, and aluminum foil. Soak for 10 to 15 minutes, then gently push off with an orangewood stick.

### 17. Is DIY gel removal safe?

Yes, as long as you don't force or scrape the gel off. Follow the standard process: lightly file the top coat → acetone soak → gentle push-off → moisturize and nourish.

### 18. Why do my nails look white after removal?

Temporary whitening or mild dryness is normal — it's from the acetone dehydrating the nail. Apply cuticle oil or nail strengthener and it should resolve within a few days. If whitening persists or nails thin noticeably, take a break from gel.

## Design and Color

### 19. How do I pick a gel color that suits my skin tone?

- **Warm/yellow undertones**: Coral, peach, caramel browns, earthy neutrals
- **Cool/pink undertones**: Berry red, grape purple, icy blue
- **Deep skin tones**: Bright orange, vivid yellow, gold — all stunning

### 20. Which colors make hands look brighter?

Pale nudes, milky white, soft lavender, and baby blue in shades slightly lighter than your skin tone. A glossy top coat maximizes the brightening effect.

### 21. Can gel nails be ombre?

Yes. Use two gel colors and blend them at the transition zone with a brush, or use a sponge to dab the gradient. Seal with a clear top coat for a smooth finish.

## Cost and Frequency

### 22. How often should I get a gel fill or new set?

Every **2 to 3 weeks** is standard, or when significant regrowth is visible at the base. Fills maintain the look; full removal and reapplication gives a cleaner result.

### 23. How much does a gel manicure cost?

Prices vary widely by location and salon. In Taiwan, basic gel manicures start around NT$600–1,500; elaborate nail art designs can reach NT$2,000–4,000+.

### 24. Are gel nails worth the cost compared to regular polish?

Regular polish typically chips within a week. Gel lasts 2 to 4 weeks and chips far less easily. In terms of longevity per dollar spent, gel manicures are generally a better value.

## Troubleshooting

### 25. Why does my gel feel tacky or sticky after curing?

Most gel polishes leave a "inhibition layer" (sticky residue) after curing. Wipe it off with isopropyl alcohol. If the whole nail still feels uncured, check your lamp wattage and cure time.

### 26. Why is my gel color patchy or uneven?

Common causes: applying too thick a coat (thin coats work better), oil residue on the nail, or insufficient base coat. Apply thin layers and cure fully between coats.

### 27. Why did bubbles appear in my gel after curing?

Applying the gel too quickly or trapping air bubbles in the product. Slow, smooth brush strokes and thorough curing between each layer solve this.

### 28. Why does my top coat lose its shine quickly?

Matte top coats are designed to be flat — that's normal. If a glossy top coat dulls quickly, it's likely being degraded by acidic substances (citrus, vinegar) or harsh cleaners.

### 29. Why does my gel keep lifting at the edges?

Make sure you're "capping" the free edge — applying a thin layer of base and color over the tip of the nail (the very edge). This seals the gel and dramatically reduces lifting.

### 30. Is it normal for gel to feel hot under the lamp?

A mild warmth is normal. Sharp or burning pain is not. If it hurts, the lamp may be too powerful, the gel coat may be too thick, or your nails may be thin. Apply thinner coats and pulse-cure (short bursts) if needed.
