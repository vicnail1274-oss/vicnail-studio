## Why This Guide Is Worth Reading

Have you ever had gel nails start lifting after just three days and not known why? Heard the term "HEMA" but had no idea what it means for your skin? Wanted to try gel nails at home but worried about damaging your natural nails?

This guide walks you through everything — from the basic science behind gel nails, to product ingredients, application technique, natural nail aftercare, and common problems with their root solutions.

## How Gel Nails Work

### What Are Gel Nails?

Gel nails (sometimes called "gel polish" or "UV gel nails") are nail products that cure under UV or LED light. Unlike regular nail polish, gel is semi-fluid before curing — it flows and self-levels — then hardens into a durable coating when exposed to light.

**Three main types:**
- **Soak-off (Soft) Gel:** Removable with acetone soak. Lasts 2–4 weeks. The most common type on the market.
- **Hard Gel:** More durable and suitable for extensions. Lasts 4–6 weeks. Must be filed off, not soaked.
- **Poly Gel:** Highly moldable, great for extensions. Lasts 4–6 weeks.

### The Chemistry: Photopolymerization

Gel contains photoinitiators — molecules that react to specific light wavelengths (UV at ~365nm or LED at ~395–410nm). When the right light hits them, they trigger a free-radical chain reaction that links the liquid molecules into a stable solid network. That's why gel needs to be "cured" under a lamp to harden.

## What Is HEMA?

### HEMA (Hydroxyethyl Methacrylate)

HEMA is a primary ingredient in many gel formulas. It improves adhesion and flexibility — but because its molecules are small and highly penetrating, it can cause contact allergies in some people. Symptoms include itching, redness, blistering around the nail, or nail bed separation.

**Important:** Allergic reactions typically develop after prolonged, repeated exposure. Many people use gel for two or three years without issue, then suddenly become sensitized — that's cumulative effect at work.

HEMA-free products substitute larger molecules theoretically less likely to penetrate skin, but long-term safety research is still limited. The most reliable approach is to use reputable brands and minimize uncured gel contact with skin — regardless of the formula.

## Application Technique: The Real Determinant of Durability

80% of lifting and peeling problems come from application technique, not product quality.

### Prep: The Most Overlooked Step

1. Shape the nail (file in one direction — no sawing)
2. Push cuticles back (soften first, push gently — don't cut aggressively)
3. Buff the nail surface (remove shine, thin by ~0.1–0.2mm)
4. Cleanse (alcohol or prep solution to remove oils)
5. Apply Dehydrator → removes surface moisture
6. Apply Primer or Bond → maximizes adhesion

### The Three-Thin-Layer Rule

- Each layer should be as thin as possible (~0.1–0.2mm)
- Minimum three layers (base + 2x color + top coat)
- **Cap the free edge:** Seal each layer over the nail tip — this is the key technique for preventing lifting
- Keep gel at least 0.5mm away from skin and sidewalls

## Natural Nail Care

### Post-Removal Recovery

After removal, the nail plate is typically dehydrated, protein-depleted, and weakened. Recommended steps:

1. Apply a nail strengthener for 3–7 days
2. Use cuticle oil daily (look for jojoba oil, vitamin E)
3. Take a 1–2 week break from nail enhancements

### Ongoing Maintenance Habits

- Wear gloves for dishes and laundry
- Minimize use of acetone-based polish removers
- Supplement with Biotin if needed — allow 3–6 months to see results

## Common Questions

### Why is my gel lifting after just a few days?

Most common causes: incomplete prep (40%), uncapped free edge (25%), naturally oily nail beds (20%), gel applied too thick (10%), insufficient lamp cure time (5%).

### Can I do gel nails at home?

Yes, but beginners should start with nail tip stickers or press-ons first. For gel at home, you need to understand proper buffing, dehydration, and thin application technique at minimum.

### Do you always have to buff the nail?

Not necessarily. Some "zero-damage" formulas use acid-based primers instead of physical buffing, but long-term results are still being studied.

## How to Evaluate a Nail Tech

- Ask if they check ingredient labels on products they use
- Observe how long prep takes (a proper prep should be at least 10–15 minutes)
- Does she ask about your nail history and condition before starting?
- Check the hygiene setup: metal tools should be sterilized; foam files should be single-use per client
