Choosing a gel color sounds easy — until you pick something gorgeous on the swatch and it looks completely off on your hand. That gap comes from the interplay of skin tone, lighting, and occasion. This guide gives you a system to find colors that actually work for you.

## Start With Your Skin Tone

### Warm/Yellow-Toned Skin (Most Common in Taiwan)

Avoid colors that amplify the yellow cast — pure yellow, orange-yellow, and mustard can make hands look dull. These work best:

- **Nude pink**: Choose a shade one step lighter than your skin for a clean, elongating effect
- **Earth tones**: Mocha, milk tea, caramel — highly compatible with warm skin
- **Deep berry shades**: Dark red, deep purple, burgundy — adds vitality to yellow-toned skin
- **Blue-green tones**: Cool colors create contrast against warm skin, adding visual interest
- **Avoid**: Pure yellow, orange, and mustard — they enhance the yellow undertone

### Fair/Cool-Toned Skin

Fair skin is versatile, but a few notes:

- **Bright, vivid colors**: Coral red, watermelon pink, mint green — vivid colors pop beautifully on fair skin
- **Pure white or cream**: Risks looking washed out — add a slight pink or warm undertone
- **Neons**: Fluorescent yellow, green, and pink are most dramatic on fair skin

### Deep/Dark Skin Tones

Deeper skin tones carry high-saturation colors exceptionally well:

- **Jewel tones**: Emerald, royal blue, deep purple — look regal against dark skin
- **Gold and bronze**: Rose gold and earth gold complement deeper skin naturally
- **Avoid**: Very light nude shades — they tend to disappear against darker skin, losing definition

## Choose by Occasion

### Work / Business Settings

- Best choices: Nude tones (light pink, milky white, rose nude)
- Also acceptable: Deep red, burgundy, dark brown
- Avoid: Neons, complex nail art, extremely long nail shapes

The guiding principle: your nails should say "put-together" before they say anything else.

### Everyday Casual

- Any color you love works here
- Great opportunity to try seasonal trend colors
- Perfect for experimenting with something outside your comfort zone

### Formal Events (Weddings, Parties)

- **As the bride**: Nude pink, light rose, pearl white — add shimmer in the same tone for elegance
- **Wedding guest**: Avoid pure white (clashes with the bride), avoid overly attention-grabbing shades
- **Party**: Bold moment — deep colors, metallics, and gradients all fit well

## Seasonal Color Direction

### Spring

- Signature shades: Macaroon pastels — lavender, milk tea pink, mint green
- Mood: High brightness, low saturation — light and fresh

### Summer

- Signature shades: Coral orange, watermelon pink, sky blue, lemon yellow
- Mood: Vivid and energetic, pairs well with summer fashion

### Autumn

- Signature shades: Pumpkin orange, maple red, caramel brown, deep burgundy
- Mood: Warm, rich, high-saturation

### Winter

- Signature shades: Foggy gray, wine red, forest green, deep navy
- Mood: Deep and refined — matte finish works especially well

## Practical Color-Selection Process

1. **Identify your undertone**: Check the inside of your wrist in natural daylight
2. **Clarify your occasion**: Where are you going this week?
3. **Note your wardrobe palette for the season**: Nails don't need to match — but they shouldn't clash
4. **Check swatches under salon lighting AND natural light**: Salon lighting varies widely
5. **Request a test swatch on your wrist**: Many salons allow this — worth asking
6. **Trust your first instinct**: Research shows initial color reactions tend to be the most accurate

## Common Mistakes

- **Choosing colors only from a phone screen**: Screen color temperature differs significantly from reality
- **Picking what looks good on someone else**: Skin tones change everything
- **Ignoring nail shape**: Short rounded nails in dark colors can make fingers look wider; long square nails in light colors maximize the elongation effect
- **Forgetting maintenance**: Dark colors and nail art show regrowth more visibly — schedule touch-ups sooner

## Closing Thoughts

There are no wrong color choices — just more informed ones. Align your skin tone, occasion, and season, then layer in your personal style. Next time you sit in front of a color swatch display, start by asking yourself: "Where am I going? What's my skin tone? What season is it?" The right color will follow.
